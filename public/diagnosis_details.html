Benign Paroxysmal Positional Vertigo (BPPV)

BPPV is a disorder arising from a problem in the inner ear, where small calcium crystals (otoconia) become dislodged and move into one of the semicircular canals. This can cause brief episodes of dizziness, especially with changes in head position.

Symptoms

  • Brief episodes of vertigo triggered by specific head movements
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness
  • Nausea or vomiting

Causes

Age-related degeneration, head injury, or inner ear conditions.

Treatment

Canalith repositioning maneuvers, vestibular rehabilitation therapy, and in rare cases, surgery.

Ménière’s Disease

A chronic condition of the inner ear that causes recurrent episodes of vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus (ringing in the ear), and a feeling of fullness in the ear. It is thought to be related to an abnormal fluid buildup in the inner ear.

Symptoms

  • Vertigo lasting 20 minutes to several hours
  • Fluctuating hearing loss
  • Tinnitus
  • A feeling of pressure or fullness in the ear

Causes

Unknown, but may involve genetic and environmental factors, infections, or autoimmune responses.

Treatment

Dietary changes (low-salt diet), diuretics, vestibular suppressants, anti-nausea medications, steroid injections, or surgery in severe cases.

Vestibular Neuritis

An inflammation of the vestibular nerve, typically caused by a viral infection, leading to sudden, severe vertigo.

Symptoms

  • Sudden onset of severe vertigo
  • Dizziness, balance difficulties
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • No hearing loss or tinnitus (distinguishing it from labyrinthitis)

Causes

Viral infections, bacterial infections, or less commonly, autoimmune disorders.

Treatment

Corticosteroids, vestibular suppressants (e.g., antihistamines, benzodiazepines), anti-nausea medications, and vestibular rehabilitation exercises.

Labyrinthitis

Inflammation of the labyrinth (the inner ear structure responsible for hearing and balance), often caused by a viral or bacterial infection.

Symptoms

  • Sudden onset of vertigo
  • Hearing loss in one ear
  • Tinnitus
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Difficulty with balance and coordination

Causes

Viral infections (most common), bacterial infections, or autoimmune reactions.

Treatment

Antiviral or antibiotic medications (depending on the cause), corticosteroids to reduce inflammation, vestibular suppressants, and vestibular rehabilitation therapy.

Vestibular Migraine

A type of migraine that includes vestibular symptoms such as vertigo or imbalance, even without a headache. It involves abnormal brain activity that affects the vestibular system.

Symptoms

  • Episodic vertigo lasting minutes to hours
  • Sensitivity to motion, light, or sound
  • Nausea, dizziness, and balance problems
  • May or may not be accompanied by a headache

Causes

Genetic factors, hormonal changes, stress, certain foods, or environmental triggers.

Treatment

Lifestyle changes (diet, sleep, and stress management), vestibular rehabilitation, and medications (e.g., beta-blockers, antidepressants, anticonvulsants)

Vestibular Schwannoma (Acoustic Neuroma)

A non-cancerous tumor that develops on the vestibular nerve leading from the inner ear to the brain. It can affect hearing and balance as it grows.

Symptoms

  • Gradual hearing loss in one ear
  • Tinnitus
  • Dizziness or unsteadiness
  • Facial numbness or weakness (if the tumor presses on nearby nerves)

Causes

Typically, a sporadic genetic mutation; in some cases, associated with a genetic condition called Neurofibromatosis Type II.

Treatment

Monitoring (for small, asymptomatic tumors), radiation therapy, or surgical removal depending on size, growth rate, and patient health.

How are vestibular disorders treated?

Your treatment depends on the specific condition. Vestibular disorder treatments may include:

  • Lifestyle changes: Conservative treatments, like lifestyle changes, go a long way in managing several vestibular disorders. For example, if fluid buildup in your ear is an issue, reducing salty foods (which cause you to retain water) may help. So can learning to identify and manage triggers that may cause a vertigo episode.
  • Medications: You may need medicines to treat what’s causing your condition or to manage symptoms. For example, you may need antibiotics to clear bacterial infections or diuretics to reduce fluid buildup in your ear. You may need anti-nausea medications or antihistamines to manage feelings of motion sickness.
  • Dix-Hallpike & McClure Pagnini: If you have BPPV, you may work with a physical therapist to help reposition misplaced structures in your inner ear with this procedure.
